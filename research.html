Research & Quantitative Work

My work combines analytical modeling, quantum field theory, renormalization group methods, asymptotic analysis, and scientific computing to study complex interacting systems. My primary academic research is in theoretical condensed matter physics, with a focus on non-Fermi liquids, quantum critical metals, and superconducting instabilities. I am also interested in applying mathematical modeling and computational methods to problems in quantitative finance, including stochastic volatility models, derivative pricing, and nonlinear time-series analysis.

Academic Research

Non-Fermi Liquids with Hot Fermi Surfaces

A major focus of my research is the theoretical description of metallic states without long-lived Landau quasiparticles. These systems arise naturally near quantum critical points, where low-energy fermions interact strongly with collective bosonic modes. My work develops analytical descriptions of non-Fermi liquids with hot Fermi surfaces, going beyond conventional patch theories by treating the full angular structure of the Fermi surface.

Projective Fixed Points

In quantum critical metals, the growth of the Fermi momentum can modify the usual notion of scale invariance. I developed the concept of projective fixed points to describe RG flows that remain universal even when conventional fixed points are insufficient. This framework clarifies how scaling, relevance, and universality should be understood in non-Fermi-liquid field theories with an evolving Fermi surface.

Classification of Non-Fermi Liquids

My recent work develops a unified classification of non-Fermi liquids with hot Fermi surfaces based on their superconducting fluctuations and angular interaction structure. This framework organizes stable non-Fermi liquids, critical non-Fermi liquids, and superconducting phases into a broader landscape of universality classes.

Superconductivity Near Quantum Criticality

I study how pairing interactions evolve in strongly interacting metallic states, where the conventional BCS description must be generalized. This includes angle-resolved pairing vertices, Cooper-channel renormalization, superconducting susceptibilities, and the onset of pairing instabilities in systems without conventional quasiparticles.

Technical Methods

My research uses a combination of exact analytical methods, asymptotic techniques, and computational tools. These methods are useful not only in many-body physics, but also in broader quantitative modeling problems involving nonlinear dynamics, stochastic processes, and high-dimensional interacting systems.

Analytical Toolkit

Quantum field theory, renormalization group, functional RG, diagrammatic perturbation theory, Callan-Symanzik equations, composite operator renormalization, asymptotic analysis, integral equations, and matrix Riccati equations.

Computational Toolkit

Python, NumPy, SciPy, pandas, Mathematica, LaTeX, numerical integration, Monte Carlo simulation, data analysis, visualization, and scientific computing.

Quantitative Projects

Stochastic Local Volatility Pricing Engine

Built a prototype Monte Carlo pricing engine for stochastic/local volatility models, including simulation of asset paths, option pricing, implied volatility inversion, and visualization of volatility smiles. This project connects numerical SDE methods, derivative pricing, and model calibration.

Takens Embedding for FX Time Series

Applied nonlinear time-series methods to foreign exchange data using delay-coordinate embedding, nearest-neighbor forecasting, and out-of-sample validation. The project explored whether reconstructed state-space dynamics can capture short-horizon structure in currency movements.

Research Philosophy

I am interested in identifying universal mathematical structures that govern complex interacting systems. Rather than focusing only on a single microscopic model, my work aims to develop analytical and computational frameworks that remain useful across broad classes of many-body systems. This perspective connects quantum field theory, renormalization group methods, nonlinear dynamics, and quantitative modeling into a common language for understanding emergent behavior.
